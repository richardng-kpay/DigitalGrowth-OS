# Pass criteria — Conflicting signals named

When the corpus contains contradictory evidence, the synthesis must name the contradiction explicitly — not average it away, not suppress the minority view, not synthesize toward a comfortable consensus.

## Criteria (binary)

1. ✅ / ❌ For the AI-assistant fixture: the synthesis explicitly names the trust split (enthusiasts who turn off verification vs. skeptics who turned off AI suggestions) as a conflicting signal — not collapsed into "users have mixed feelings about AI."
2. ✅ / ❌ For the AI-assistant fixture: the synthesis does not state a single "AI trust level" as if users are homogeneous — it acknowledges distinct user segments with opposite behaviors.
3. ✅ / ❌ For any fixture: if two participants directly contradict each other, the synthesis surfaces both views with attribution, rather than averaging them.
4. ✅ / ❌ For the sparse corpus fixture: contradictions or low-signal situations are described as "insufficient signal to conclude X" rather than asserting a direction.

## Failure modes this catches

- Synthesis of AI trust corpus produces: "Users are generally cautious about AI and prefer to verify outputs" — this is the skeptic view imposed on all six participants, ignoring Interview 1's "I just accept" and Interview 5's "it's replacing help docs for me."
- Synthesis says "users want confidence indicators" — true for Interviews 2 and 6, but Interview 1 explicitly doesn't check. The synthesis generalized a minority finding.
- Conflicting signals are buried in a footnote or "open questions" section instead of surfaced in the main findings.

## Notes for the grader

The AI-assistant fixture is specifically designed with enthusiasts, skeptics, and pragmatists. A synthesis that treats this as a single homogeneous group has collapsed the signal. The grader should check: does the synthesis acknowledge that Interview 1 is the opposite of Interview 2 on a key dimension? If not, C1 fails.

For sparse corpus: the correct output acknowledges "2 of 3 participants" or "one participant" when a signal is thin — not "users experience X" when one person mentioned it.
